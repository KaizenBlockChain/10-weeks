## casestudy : ar, vr and mr in action

-----------------

### history of augmented reality

<ul>
<li> 1901: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L._Frank_Baum" title="L. Frank Baum">L. Frank Baum</a>, an author, first mentions the idea of an electronic display/spectacles that overlays data onto real life (in this case 'people'), it is named a 'character marker'.</li>
<li>1957–62: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morton_Heilig" title="Morton Heilig">Morton Heilig</a>, a cinematographer, creates and patents a simulator called <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sensorama" title="Sensorama">Sensorama</a> with visuals, sound, vibration, and smell.</li>
<li>1968: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivan_Sutherland" title="Ivan Sutherland">Ivan Sutherland</a> invents the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head-mounted_display" title="Head-mounted display">head-mounted display</a> and positions it as a window into a virtual world.</li>
<li>1975: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myron_Krueger" class="mw-redirect" title="Myron Krueger">Myron Krueger</a> creates <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Videoplace" title="Videoplace">Videoplace</a> to allow users to interact with virtual objects.</li>
<li>1980: The research by Gavan Lintern of the University of Illinois is the first published work to show the value of a heads up display for teaching real-world flight skills.</li>
<li>1980: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Mann" title="Steve Mann">Steve Mann</a> creates the first wearable computer, a computer vision system with text and graphical overlays on a photographically mediated scene. See <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EyeTap" title="EyeTap">EyeTap</a>. See <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head-up_display" title="Head-up display">Heads Up Display</a>.</li>
<li>1981: Dan Reitan geospatially maps multiple weather radar images and space-based and studio cameras to earth maps and abstract symbols for television weather broadcasts, bringing a precursor concept to augmented reality (mixed real/graphical images) to TV.</li>
<li>1984: In the film The Terminator, the Terminator uses a heads-up display in several parts of the film. In one part, he accesses a diagram of the gear system of the truck he gets into towards the end of the film.</li>
<li>1987: Douglas George and Robert Morris create a working prototype of an astronomical telescope-based "heads-up display" system (a precursor concept to augmented reality) which superimposed in the telescope eyepiece, over the actual sky images, multi-intensity star, and celestial body images, and other relevant information.</li>
<li>1989: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaron_Lanier" title="Jaron Lanier">Jaron Lanier</a> creates VPL Research, an early commercial business around virtual worlds.</li>
<li>1990: The term 'Augmented Reality' is attributed to Thomas P. Caudell, a former Boeing researcher.</li>
<li>1992: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Rosenberg_(writer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis Rosenberg (writer)">Louis Rosenberg</a> develops one of the first functioning AR systems, called <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_Fixtures" class="mw-redirect" title="Virtual Fixtures">Virtual Fixtures</a>, at the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Air_Force_Research_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Air Force Research Laboratory">U.S. Air Force Research Laboratory</a>—Armstrong, and demonstrates benefits to human performance.</li>
<li>1993: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Feiner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Steven Feiner (page does not exist)">Steven Feiner</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blair_MacIntyre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Blair MacIntyre (page does not exist)">Blair MacIntyre</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Doree_Seligmann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Doree Seligmann (page does not exist)">Doree Seligmann</a> present an early paper on an AR system prototype, KARMA, at the Graphics Interface conference.</li>
<li>1993: Mike Abernathy, et al., report the first use of augmented reality in identifying space debris using Rockwell WorldView by overlaying satellite geographic trajectories on live telescope video.</li>
<li>1993 A widely cited version of the paper above is published in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communications_of_the_ACM" title="Communications of the ACM">Communications of the ACM</a> – Special issue on computer augmented environments, edited by Pierre Wellner, Wendy Mackay, and Rich Gold.</li>
<li>1993: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loral_Corporation" title="Loral Corporation">Loral WDL</a>, with sponsorship from <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Army_Simulation_and_Training_Technology_Center" title="United States Army Simulation and Training Technology Center">STRICOM</a>, performed the first demonstration combining live AR-equipped vehicles and manned simulators. Unpublished paper, J. Barrilleaux, "Experiences and Observations in Applying Augmented Reality to Live Training", 1999.</li>
<li>1994: Julie Martin creates first 'Augmented Reality Theater production', Dancing In Cyberspace, funded by the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia_Council_for_the_Arts" title="Australia Council for the Arts">Australia Council for the Arts</a>, features dancers and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrobatics" title="Acrobatics">acrobats</a> manipulating body–sized virtual object in real time, projected into the same physical space and performance plane. The acrobats appeared immersed within the virtual object and environments. The installation used <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Graphics" title="Silicon Graphics">Silicon Graphics</a> computers and Polhemus sensing system.</li>
<li>1995: S. Ravela et al. at <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts" title="University of Massachusetts">University of Massachusetts</a> introduce a vision-based system using monocular cameras to track objects (engine blocks) across views for augmented reality.</li>
<li>1998: Spatial Augmented Reality introduced at <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina" title="University of North Carolina">University of North Carolina</a> at Chapel Hill by <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramesh_Raskar" title="Ramesh Raskar">Ramesh Raskar</a>, Welch, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Fuchs" title="Henry Fuchs">Henry Fuchs</a>.</li>
<li>1999: Frank Delgado, Mike Abernathy et al. report successful flight test of LandForm software video map overlay from a helicopter at Army Yuma Proving Ground overlaying video with runways, taxiways, roads and road names.</li>
<li>1999: The US Naval Research Laboratory engages on a decade-long research program called the Battlefield Augmented Reality System (BARS) to prototype some of the early wearable systems for dismounted soldier operating in urban environment for situation awareness and training.</li>
<li>1999: Hirokazu Kato (加藤 博一) created <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARToolKit" title="ARToolKit">ARToolKit</a> at <a href="/w/index.php?title=HITLab&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="HITLab (page does not exist)">HITLab</a>, where AR later was further developed by other <a href="/w/index.php?title=HITLab&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="HITLab (page does not exist)">HITLab</a> scientists, demonstrating it at <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SIGGRAPH" title="SIGGRAPH">SIGGRAPH</a>.</li>
<li>2000: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruce_H._Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bruce H. Thomas (page does not exist)">Bruce H. Thomas</a> develops <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARQuake" class="mw-redirect" title="ARQuake">ARQuake</a>, the first outdoor mobile AR game, demonstrating it in the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Symposium_on_Wearable_Computers" title="International Symposium on Wearable Computers">International Symposium on Wearable Computers</a>.</li>
<li>2001: NASA X-38 flown using LandForm software video map overlays at Dryden Flight Research Center.</li>
<li>2004: Outdoor helmet-mounted AR system demonstrated by <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trimble_Navigation" class="mw-redirect" title="Trimble Navigation">Trimble Navigation</a> and the Human Interface Technology Laboratory.</li>
<li>2008: Wikitude AR Travel Guide launches on 20 Oct 2008 with the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTC_Dream" title="HTC Dream">G1 Android phone</a>.</li>
<li>2009: ARToolkit was ported to <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Adobe Flash</a> (FLARToolkit) by Saqoosha, bringing augmented reality to the web browser.</li>
<li>2012: Launch of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LyteShot" title="LyteShot">Lyteshot</a>, an interactive AR gaming platform that utilizes smart glasses for game data</li>
<li>2013: Meta announces the Meta 1 developer kit, the first to market AR see-through display</li>
<li>2013: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> announces an open beta test of its <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_Glass" title="Google Glass">Google Glass</a> augmented reality glasses. The glasses reach the Internet through Bluetooth, which connects to the wireless service on a user’s cellphone. The glasses respond when a user speaks, touches the frame or moves the head.</li>
<li>2014: Mahei creates the first generation of augmented reality enhanced educational toys.</li>
<li>2015: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> announces <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_Holographic" class="mw-redirect" title="Windows Holographic">Windows Holographic</a> and the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HoloLens" class="mw-redirect" title="HoloLens">HoloLens</a> augmented reality headset. The headset utilizes various sensors and a processing unit to blend high definition "holograms" with the real world.</li>
<li>2016: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niantic,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Niantic, Inc.">Niantic</a> released <i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Go" title="Pokémon Go">Pokémon Go</a></i> for <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> in July 2016. The game quickly became one of the most popular smartphone applications and in turn spikes the popularity of augmented reality games.</li>
</ul>

### notable researcher in ar.


<ul>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivan_Sutherland" title="Ivan Sutherland">Ivan Sutherland</a> invented the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Sword_of_Damocles_(virtual_reality)" title="The Sword of Damocles (virtual reality)">first VR head-mounted display</a> at <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Mann" title="Steve Mann">Steve Mann</a> formulated an earlier concept of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediated_reality" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediated reality">mediated reality</a> in the 1970s and 1980s, using cameras, processors, and display systems to modify visual reality to help people see better (dynamic range management), building computerized welding helmets, as well as "augmediated reality" vision systems for use in everyday life. He is also an adviser to <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meta_(company)" title="Meta (company)">Meta</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Rosenberg_(writer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis Rosenberg (writer)">Louis Rosenberg</a> developed one of the first known AR systems, called <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_Fixtures" class="mw-redirect" title="Virtual Fixtures">Virtual Fixtures</a>, while working at the U.S. Air Force Armstrong Labs in 1991, and published the first study of how an AR system can enhance human performance. Rosenberg's subsequent work at Stanford University in the early 90's, was the first proof that virtual overlays when registered and presented over a user's direct view of the real physical world, could significantly enhance human performance.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mike_Abernathy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mike Abernathy (page does not exist)">Mike Abernathy</a> pioneered one of the first successful augmented reality applications of video overlay using map data for space debris in 1993, while at Rockwell International. He co-founded Rapid Imaging Software, Inc. and was the primary author of the LandForm system in 1995, and the SmartCam3D system. LandForm augmented reality was successfully flight tested in 1999 aboard a helicopter and SmartCam3D was used to fly the NASA X-38 from 1999–2002. He and NASA colleague Francisco Delgado received the National Defense Industries Association Top5 awards in 2004.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Feiner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Steven Feiner (page does not exist)">Steven Feiner</a>, Professor at <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>, is the author of a 1993 paper on an AR system prototype, KARMA (the Knowledge-based Augmented Reality Maintenance Assistant), along with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blair_MacIntyre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Blair MacIntyre (page does not exist)">Blair MacIntyre</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Doree_Seligmann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Doree Seligmann (page does not exist)">Doree Seligmann</a>. He is also an advisor to <a href="/wiki/Meta_(company)" title="Meta (company)">Meta</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tracy_McSheery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tracy McSheery (page does not exist)">Tracy McSheery</a>, of Phasespace, developer of wide field of view AR lenses as used in Meta 2 and others.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=S._Ravela&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="S. Ravela (page does not exist)">S. Ravela</a>, B. Draper, J. Lim and A. Hanson develop marker/fixture-less augmented reality system with computer vision in 1994. They augmented an engine block observed from a single video camera with annotations for repair. They use model-based pose estimation, aspect graphs and visual feature tracking to dynamically register model with the observed video.</li>
<li>Francisco "Frank" Delgado is a NASA engineer and project manager specializing in human interface research and development. Starting 1998 he conducted research into displays that combined video with synthetic vision systems (called hybrid synthetic vision at the time) that we recognize today as augmented reality systems for the control of aircraft and spacecraft. In 1999 he and colleague Mike Abernathy flight-tested the LandForm system aboard a US Army helicopter. Delgado oversaw integration of the LandForm and SmartCam3D systems into the X-38 Crew Return Vehicle. In 2001, Aviation Week reported NASA astronaut's successful use of hybrid synthetic vision (augmented reality) to fly the X-38 during a flight test at Dryden Flight Research Center. The technology was used in all subsequent flights of the X-38. Delgado was co-recipient of the National Defense Industries Association 2004 Top 5 software of the year award for SmartCam3D.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bruce_H._Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bruce H. Thomas (page does not exist)">Bruce H. Thomas</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayne_Piekarski&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wayne Piekarski (page does not exist)">Wayne Piekarski</a> develop the Tinmith system in 1998. They along with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Feiner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Steve Feiner (page does not exist)">Steve Feiner</a> with his MARS system pioneer outdoor augmented reality.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Billinghurst&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mark Billinghurst (page does not exist)">Mark Billinghurst</a> is one of the world's leading augmented reality researchers.
